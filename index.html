Dear team at CUE,

I am applying for a student research assistant position at your chair. My interest in CUE's work stems from my ongoing engagement with the ecological and social dimensions of urban space — an interest that has shaped my studies, my activism, and my practice from the very beginning.

Over the course of my studies, it became increasingly clear to me that architecture is not only a design discipline, but a tool for making social structures visible and helping to reshape them. For several semesters I have been involved in the student collective perspektiv;wechsel, which I co-founded. Our lecture series and events engage critically with intersectional feminist questions of urban perception, reception, and power relations in the built environment. This work has strongly shaped my thinking and my design practice — and shown me how productive collective processes can be for architectural discourse and practice.

I do not see design, organisation, and politics as separate fields, but as mutually entangled levels of responsibility. In my current position as a working student at ZRS Architekten, I am involved in construction documentation and have developed a strong interest in questions of material-appropriate construction, building surveys, and damage mapping. Alongside this, I am working in my Master's programme on cartographic and spatio-temporal methods for analysing urban ecosystems — most recently through a transect of the Panketalniederung.

Projects such as the Böden Berlins seminar (SoSe 2025) show that spatio-temporal soil mapping is established as a critical practice at TU Berlin. My transect through the Panketalniederung connects to these questions — focusing on hydrological history, land ownership, and ecological habitability.

Beyond feminist urbanism, sustainability is a topic I continuously engage with. The construction sector bears significant responsibility for anthropogenic climate change and is urgently in need of reform. In the future, I want to dedicate myself to exactly this task and work towards more sustainable cities.

I look forward very much to the possibility of becoming part of the discourse at CUE.

Sincerely,
Lara Luise Brenner

Space Time Transect

with Eliott Croubalian  ·  SS 2026  ·  Semester project, ongoing

In our research, the Panke River serves as a primary example of a territory in constant transformation. Shaped first by glacial melt, the landscape remains in motion as the river would slowly erode its own path. We observed that human attempts to freeze these meanders in time, in the name of efficiency, often backfire, because the landscape resists static control. This led us to a shift in perspective: we no longer see the territory as a fixed condition, but as an entropic mixture. To map this entropy, we have to understand time as an active physical force, one that produces both decay and accumulation.

To make this process legible, we first organized the terrain through a three-dimensional matrix of geography, history, and condition. This method worked well in pairs: history and geography could produce palimpsests, while history and condition could be traced through datasets and chronological shifts. But when all three axes were combined, the center of the matrix collapsed into a void. The result was what we came to call an “overlay overload,” in which too many layers rendered the drawing unreadable.

PRESS HERE TO PLAY

To address this problem, we developed the space-time transect. This instrument rests on two conceptual operations. First, we flatten spatial depth: the vertical space of a traditional map is compressed until geography becomes a single horizontal line of distance, x. Then we replace the vertical axis with time, t. In this way, we give up spatial depth in order to gain temporal depth. The result is a map that allows historical events and material conditions to be grasped with an intuitive understanding of what we are already used to from reading space-dimensional maps. We imagine Geological strata, ownership patterns, and hydrological traces along a timeline can then be understood and made accessible.

Midterm status, SS 2026. The project is still very much a work in Progress

As it rains, the soil gets wet

with Manuel Rademaker

Es regnet, es regnet,
die Erde wird nass!
Und wenn's genug geregnet hat,
dann wächst auch wieder Gras!

(German nursery rhyme)

Es regnet, die Erde wird nass.
I’m sitting here looking at this exposed patch of ground.
No Thing, no plan, only a beginning.
The ground is open again.
It could absorb water, hold roots, regulate temperature.
Already, a thin green film is spreading across the surface.
Accomplished through subtraction rather than addition.
The removed material lies beside me—enough to sit, rest, and stay.
Nothing here is complete.
No promises were given. It is a simple principle:
Exposing not condensing.
As it rains, the soil gets wet.
